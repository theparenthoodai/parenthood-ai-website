The Nap Window

PUBLISHED · JULY 2026

I've been back in it for a few months now. Properly back, after taking time off to move. And what I've noticed is that all I want to do is sit at a computer and learn.

That's the honest version. Not "I'm trying to stay current." Not "I feel like I should keep up." I want to. It's the most interesting thing I've found in a long time, and given a free hour I will choose it over almost anything else in the house.

Which is where the problem starts.

There are two windows

Nap time, and after bedtime. Ninety minutes each on a good day, and a good day is not most days.

In that time I can clean the kitchen. Or clean the bathrooms. Or cook something for tomorrow so that tomorrow is easier. Or sleep, because I'm tired, and being tired is not a small thing when there are two small people relying on you. Or I can learn.

I get one. Whatever I pick, the others don't happen.

And it isn't just learning, either. That's the part I underestimated. You can't only read about this stuff. You have to practice. Put in the reps. Build something, break it, fix it, think of a use case, try it, watch what other people are making and work out how they did it. It's not a podcast you can absorb while folding laundry. It needs both hands and a working brain, and both of those are in short supply by eight in the evening.

The part that makes it hard

The scheduling isn't the hard bit. The hard bit is that every hour has an opportunity cost, and the cost is usually a person I love.

An hour on the laptop is an hour not with my kids, or not with my husband, or not asleep next to him. It's an hour the kitchen stays how it is. Nobody's angry about it. It's not a fight. It just sits there quietly in the background of the choice.

And here's the bit I think a lot of parents feel and won't say out loud: sometimes I want the laptop more than I want the nap. More than I want the clean kitchen. I'm not choosing it because I'm disciplined or because I'm building toward something noble. I'm choosing it because I like it. That's harder to admit than being busy.

I'm not writing this to complain. I chose all of this, and I'd choose it again. I'm writing it because I kept seeing the same feeling everywhere and never seeing anyone name the cost.

So I stopped treating them as two separate things

This is the only thing I've found that actually works, and it isn't a productivity trick. It's a rule about what I'm allowed to spend the window on.

The rule is that whatever I'm learning has to be applicable to my actual life. Not a tutorial project. Not a demo I'll never open again. If I'm going to spend a nap window building something, it has to also take something off my plate.

Because then the window does two jobs. I get the reps in, and the house gets easier. That's the intersection, and it's the entire reason parenthood.ai exists.

One example, and I know it's the most common one there is, so I'll keep it short. Meal planning used to be a genuine weekly stress. I've tried the boxes. I've tried saving recipes. I've tried the binder thing that everyone online swears by. What I actually did most weeks was stare into the fridge, then go to the grocery store, then come home with a hundred dollars of things that don't make a meal.

So I built a workflow instead. I photograph the fridge, the freezer, the pantry, and hand it over with one instruction: use what's already here first. It gives me meals from what I own, my grocery bill went down, and less food spoils.

But look at what that cost me. I had to learn how to build a workflow. Learn to connect things together. Learn to make it search local flyers. Build it, test it, break it, fix it. That was several nap windows of proper learning, and at the end of it I had both. The skill, and one less thing on my plate forever.

That's the trade I'm looking for now. Not "how do I find more time to learn." How do I make the learning pay rent.

The floor

I want to be honest about the limit of this, because I don't think it solves the problem. It just makes it survivable.

Until you can pay someone to clean your house and cook your meals, something gives. That's not a mindset issue you can think your way out of. It's arithmetic. There are a fixed number of hours and a non-negotiable number of jobs, and no amount of AI removes a toddler from the room.

So you're going to feel behind. Not because you're lazy, and not because you didn't optimize hard enough. Because you have responsibilities that don't care about your learning goals.

What I've stopped doing is treating that feeling as evidence that I'm failing. It isn't. It's just what it costs to be doing two things at once, at this stage, with these people in the house.

The window is ninety minutes. It's not going to get longer for a few years yet. All I've worked out is how to make it count twice.
